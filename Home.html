Audiobooks:
The (in)visible labor behind the boom.

By Ruth Livier/UCLA PhD student, Information Studies/April 12, 2019

This project participates in recuperating lost and/or understudied historical experiences and contributions of U.S. Latinas to U.S. audio history.

Abstract

Audiobook narration is an area of digital labor both facilitated and complicated by technological advances. Audiobooks bridge issues of technology, Library and Information Studies, labor, unions, disability studies, copyright issues, transnationalism and globalization, among others. Studies of this ecosystem, already few and far between, tend to be told from a neoliberal perspective. My research shifts the conversation to issues of labor—both visible and invisible and studies this growing industry from the workers’ perspective. It, additionally, asks what new insights we might gain if analyzing this ecosystem and audiobook narration from the intersections of Latina feminist studies, information studies, and labor. My aim is to unravel how audiobook narration—as digitally mediated creative work—is being constructed as a form of precarious labor and how this precarity is distributed along racial and gendered lines.
